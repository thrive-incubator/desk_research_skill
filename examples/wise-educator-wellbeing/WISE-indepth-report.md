# Stage 1 Desk Research: WISE Educator Wellbeing

_A spin-out of the consultation, credentialing, and capacity-building layer of Georgetown/MedStar WISE's tiered (MTSS-style) educator-wellbeing framework, sold to K-12 districts and states. Recommendation: CONDITIONAL GO · Likely path: spin-out with guardrails._

## Executive Summary

The why-now for WISE Educator Wellbeing is real, dated, and unusually sharp. Educator burnout is a structurally embedded, expensive problem; the federal money that propped up school mental health for three years has collapsed on a hard timeline (the ESSER obligation deadline passed in September 2024, and ~$1B in Bipartisan Safer Communities Act school-mental-health grants was cancelled in April 2025 and only partly restored as a $208M round by December 2025); and the school-based clinical workforce sits in a 20-year hole (1,071 students per school psychologist against a recommended 500). A framework that helps districts *map and build local capacity* rather than *add headcount they can no longer fund* is well-timed to that squeeze.

The mission fit is excellent. This is squarely Thrive's "children's mental health and the workforce that supports it," expressed in its most leverage-rich form: move the adults, and the students move with them. The honest market read says the venture sizes like a sustainable, low-to-mid **$3–15M ARR** business — the studio's exact posture — provided the consultation is productized and a credentialing royalty layer is stood up; a pure bespoke-consulting version is labor-bound and tops out below that band.

But three of the venture's self-descriptions do not survive contact with the public record, and they define what Stage 2 has to settle:

1. **"No competitor at scale" is not true.** The field is fragmented, not empty. Well-funded players (Effective School Solutions, Cartwheel, Care Solace, Hazel Health, Panorama) own the *student* mental-health budget, and a separate, more fragmented set (RISE/Kaiser Thriving Schools, Consult4Ed, CharacterStrong, plus free CASEL and Greater Good content) already sells *educator/staff* wellbeing. WISE's wedge — organizational/relational-health diagnosis with clinical depth and Georgetown research authority — is real and defensible, but it has to be named against this set, not asserted into a vacuum.
2. **The headline evidence claim is not publicly verifiable.** The claim that the framework "moves the needle on educator wellbeing *and* positively impacts school culture and student wellbeing with no direct student intervention" does not appear as a published, peer-reviewed study in the public record — only program-reported reach (≈40 DC schools, 14,000 students, 1,800 staff in 2023–24) and local media. For a venture whose entire moat is research authority, closing this gap is the single most important pre-Stage-3 task.
3. **Willingness-to-pay is grant-shaped, and the strongest cited proof point doesn't verify as WISE.** Inbound demand is genuine, but every named buyer is in a *funding-seeking* motion, not a signed recurring contract. The "already statewide in Massachusetts" claim, on inspection, describes a *state-government-run* system (MA DESE's SEB Academy; the Massachusetts School Mental Health Consortium) that is as much a competitor template as a WISE deployment.

None of these is fatal, and all three are cheap to test. **Recommendation: CONDITIONAL GO to Stage 2 (customer discovery + prototyping). Likely path: spin-out with guardrails — but spin-in must be ruled out deliberately, not by default, given how dependent the offering is on ongoing Georgetown/MedStar clinical delivery and the fact that the WISE brand and likely the IP sit under MedStar.**

## 1. The Problem & Why Now

### The problem (current data)

Educator distress is real and chronic — but, importantly for the framing, it is currently *easing from its pandemic peak, not worsening*. In RAND's 2025 *State of the American Teacher* survey, 53% of teachers reported burnout (down from 60% in 2024) and 16% intended to leave their jobs (down from 22% in 2024). Teachers still report frequent job-related stress and burnout at roughly twice the rate of comparable working adults, and work about 49 hours per week — roughly 10 above their contracted hours ([RAND, 2025](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA1108-16.html); [RAND, 2024](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA1108-12.html)).

The skeptic's note on this: the acute emergency is receding. A pitch built on a worsening-crisis narrative will age badly and is partly contradicted by the data. The durable framing is *structural and financial* — chronic turnover, persistent vacancies, and the cost of a revolving door — not a crisis that is, on the numbers, abating.

### Cost of inaction (the buyer's frame)

This is the strongest buyer hook, because it puts a dollar figure on a line item a CFO already owns. The Learning Policy Institute's September 2024 update estimates per-teacher replacement costs of $11,860 in small districts (under 10,000 students), $16,450 in medium districts (10,000–50,000), and $24,930 in large districts (over 50,000), and puts the national bill at roughly $2.2B per year ([LPI, Sept 2024](https://learningpolicyinstitute.org/product/2024-whats-cost-teacher-turnover-factsheet)). A mid-size district losing 40 teachers a year is burning on the order of $650K in replacement costs alone — before counting the learning loss and culture damage LPI notes its estimate excludes. That number is the natural denominator for an ROI pitch: a retention-focused engagement only has to move turnover by a few teachers to pay for itself.

### Research base for the thesis

The venture's core claim — that *relational and organizational health is a protective factor*, so educators stay in hard, underpaid jobs when they feel seen, in relationship, and in meaning — is well-supported in the independent retention literature. Working conditions, administrative support, and school climate consistently rival or exceed pay as drivers of whether teachers stay ([LPI turnover research](https://learningpolicyinstitute.org/product/teacher-turnover-report)). This half of the thesis is on solid ground.

The *second* half — a causal chain from educator wellbeing to *student* wellbeing with no direct student intervention — is plausible and directionally supported by school-climate research, but it is the part WISE must own with its own evidence rather than borrow from the field. The U.S. Department of Education's own 2024 review treats educator wellbeing as an emerging, under-evidenced intervention area ([ED, Aug 2024](https://www.ed.gov/media/document/eirteacher-wellbeing508-107807.pdf)), which cuts both ways: it is white space, but it is white space precisely because the evidence base is still thin. See §2 on the gap between WISE's specific claim and the public record.

### Why now — the inflection

The funding collapse the brief points to is the genuine catalyst, and it is specific and dated:

- **The ESSER cliff.** Districts had to obligate pandemic-relief funds by September 2024; more than a third of local-education-agency spending plans had used late ESSER dollars on mental-health staff, and districts have faced budget declines on the order of 5–8% as the money ran out ([IDRA](https://www.idra.org/education_policy/what-you-need-to-know-about-the-esser-funding-cliff/); [McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/education/our-insights/when-the-money-runs-out-k-12-schools-brace-for-stimulus-free-budgets)).
- **The BSCA grant cancellation.** The Education Department cancelled roughly $1B across the School-Based Mental Health Services and Mental Health Service Professional Demonstration grant programs in April 2025 — programs supporting 260 recipients across 49 states and a pipeline of ~14,000 professionals — then restored a smaller $208M round by December 2025 ([NPR, May 2025](https://www.npr.org/2025/05/01/nx-s1-5382582/trump-school-mental-health); [EdWeek, Apr 2025](https://www.edweek.org/policy-politics/trump-ends-1-billion-in-mental-health-grants-for-schools/2025/04); [EdWeek, Dec 2025](https://www.edweek.org/policy-politics/funding-ends-for-school-mental-health-projects-after-a-roller-coaster-year/2025/12)).
- **The workforce shortage.** NASP reports a 1,071-to-1 student-to-school-psychologist ratio for 2024–25 against a recommended 500-to-1, and estimates a need for roughly 45,000–63,000 more school psychologists — a gap it projects will take more than 20 years to close ([NASP, 2024–25](https://www.nasponline.org/about-school-psychology/state-shortages-data-dashboard)).

The inflection is double-edged, and this is the central strategic tension of the whole venture: the same collapse that *motivates* districts to seek a do-more-with-less framework also *shrinks the budget that pays for it*. A model premised on building local capacity and mapping existing partnerships is the right response to that squeeze — but the buyer is, by definition, cash-constrained, which is exactly why willingness-to-pay (§5, §8) is the first thing to prove.

## 2. What the Public Record Shows

**WISE is real and substantive — and it is a MedStar brand, not a Thrive/Georgetown one.** "Wellbeing in School Environments" operates as the MedStar Georgetown University Hospital WISE Center ([medstarwise.org](https://www.medstarwise.org/)), staffed by psychologists, psychiatrists, and social workers delivering tiered, whole-school supports. Reported reach for 2023–24 was approximately 40 DC schools, 14,000 students and families, and 1,800 school staff ([MedStar 2025 report](https://bluetoad.com/article/Bringing+mental+wellbeing+support+to+students+and+teachers./4983827/846946/article.html)). The studio lead (Anna Jesseman) sits on the MedStar side (anna@medstarwise.org). The implication matters for the deal structure: the brand, and likely the core IP, live under *MedStar*, not the Thrive Center — a real institutional seam for any Georgetown/Thrive spin-out (see §6).

**TeacherWISE is confirmed.** WISE and the Georgetown University Center for Child and Human Development (now the Thrive Center) launched TeacherWISE, a research-informed online curriculum spanning five wellbeing domains ([Student Behavior Blog](https://studentbehaviorblog.org/teacherwise-well-being-notebook-from-wise-georgetown/); [MedStar WISE training](https://www.medstarwise.org/training)). This matches the brief's "asynchronous resources (TeacherWISE — built in collaboration with Thrive faculty)."

**Education Plus Health is confirmed — but it is a partner/channel, not a WISE payor for educator wellbeing.** EPH is a Philadelphia nonprofit (established 2009) running school-based health centers in roughly 15 schools serving about 7,000 youth, financially cost-neutral through per-school service contracts plus federal aid, insurance reimbursement, and philanthropy such as a [KCRF grant](https://kcrfund.org/grants-2024/education-plus-health/) ([educationplushealth.com](https://educationplushealth.com/); [Chalkbeat, Mar 2024](https://www.chalkbeat.org/philadelphia/2024/03/05/education-officials-want-more-school-based-health-centers/)). "WISE written into their plan" is credible as a *delivery partnership*, but EPH's model is student-health-center-centric and explicitly not flush — so willingness-to-pay specifically for the *educator-wellbeing* layer has to be confirmed directly with EPH, not assumed from the relationship.

**The "statewide in Massachusetts" claim does not verify as WISE-delivered.** Massachusetts does run statewide tiered school-mental-health infrastructure — MA DESE's SEB Academy (190 schools, 80 districts, 260,000-plus students) and the Massachusetts School Mental Health Consortium ([MA DESE](https://www.doe.mass.edu/sfs/mental-wellness/default.html); [MASMHC](https://masmhc.org/)) — but the public record attributes it to the *state and its consortium*, not to WISE. Either WISE delivers a component the public record does not name (confirm internally) or the brief is conflating WISE's *model* with a *state-run system that is actually a competitor/incumbent template*. Treat as unverified and important.

**Utah, Indiana, and Montana State University are unverified publicly.** No public trace of WISE in their proposals surfaced, which is normal for in-progress grant applications. Take these as authoritative internal context, but recognize they are *funding-seeking* motions, not contracts.

**The headline evidence claim is unverified in the public record.** No published, peer-reviewed study surfaced demonstrating that WISE's framework improves school culture and student wellbeing without direct student intervention. There is genuine program-reported impact and media coverage, but not the independent evidence the venture's research-authority positioning rests on. This is the most important verification gap in the report; it may well exist internally or be in progress, and confirming it with Megan is step one of Stage 2.

**Brand and naming risk is high.** "WISE" is heavily contested. The brand is currently MedStar's, and "WISE" collides with the World Innovation Summit for Education (WISE / Qatar Foundation), Women in Science & Engineering, and various financial-literacy "WISE" programs, among others. A national for-profit spin-out named "WISE" faces trademark, SEO, and brand-ownership problems all at once — directly analogous to the Attune → Attunify rename. Budget for a renaming and brand-license exercise in Stage 2.

## 3. Market Sizing

US K-12 baseline: approximately 100,000 public schools, 18,000 districts, 49.5M students (fall 2023), and 3.2M FTE teachers ([NCES CCD 2023–24](https://nces.ed.gov/ccd/tables/202324_summary_2.asp)). The sizing below is value-based (revenue); the SOM is the decision-relevant number and is built bottom-up, because the buyer here is a finite, countable set of districts and states.

| Layer | Estimate | Basis | Confidence |
|-------|----------|-------|-----------|
| TAM | ~$4–8B/yr | US external K-12 PD market (~$4.1B, 2020) + US slice of the SEL market (~$3.6–4.2B, 2023–25, ~40% North America) + school-MH services spend | Low–Med |
| SAM | ~$300–600M/yr | The share addressable by a virtual consultation + PD + credentialing model: districts/states actively buying staff-wellbeing/MTSS support, US-only, virtually deliverable | Low |
| SOM (1–3 yr) | ~$3–9M/yr | Bottom-up: see scenarios below | Med |

**Top-down anchors.** The US external professional-development market was about $4.1B in 2020 ([Statista](https://www.statista.com/statistics/790667/k-12-professional-development-market-size/)), and in-service PD runs around 3.5% of district budgets ([EdWeek, Oct 2025](https://www.edweek.org/leadership/is-spending-on-professional-development-keeping-pace/2025/10)). The social-emotional-learning market is estimated at roughly $3.6–4.2B in 2023–25 with North America about 40% of it, growing ~24% annually ([Grand View](https://www.grandviewresearch.com/industry-analysis/social-emotional-learning-sel-market-report)). Educator-wellbeing specifically is a fraction of these adjacent pools — there is no clean published figure for it, which is itself a finding.

**Bottom-up SOM — the number that matters.** The brief's own "100+ schools conservatively wanting the framework" is the anchor. Two revenue motions, which combine rather than compete:

| Motion | Assumptions | 1–3 yr revenue |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| District/school consultation | 100 schools × $25K–$75K per school/yr (implementation consultation + PD + Tier-2 support), or 15–30 districts × $150K–$400K | $2.5M–$7.5M |
| Clinician credentialing/network | A "WISE-certified consultant" credential at $1.5K–$5K per clinician × a few hundred clinicians/yr, plus recurring licensing/renewal | $0.3M–$1.5M |

The per-school and per-district figures are benchmarked against the $4K–$11.6M-per-district range districts already spend on mental-health services and the Care Solace and Cartwheel district contracts in §4; the credential figure is benchmarked against IECMH certificate pricing such as [UMN's $375/credit](https://online.umn.edu/infant-and-early-childhood-mental-health-certificate).

**Reconciliation and the honest read.** Top-down and bottom-up agree that this is a $3–15M-ARR-shaped business at the low-to-middle of the band — and, critically, one that does *not* require a venture-scale raise, which is exactly the studio's posture. The risk is the opposite of unicorn-chasing: a pure bespoke-consulting model is labor-bound and tops out well below the band. The credentialing/royalty layer and AI-leveraged delivery scaffolding are what move it from "services shop" to "sustainable product company." Growth drivers: the funding squeeze forcing do-more-with-less, the workforce shortage, state MTSS mandates, and rising attention to staff (not just student) wellbeing. Contracting forces: the same budget squeeze on the buyer side, an easing burnout curve, and grant dependence.

## 4. Competitive Landscape

The brief implies there is no real competitor at scale. The public record shows a field that is fragmented rather than empty — which is a more useful and more honest finding, because it tells WISE exactly where it has to differentiate.

### Direct and near-direct competitors

| Org | Model | Scale | Pricing posture | Strength | Weakness vs. WISE |
|-----|-------|-------|-----------------|----------|-------------------|
| Effective School Solutions | In-district clinical programs for highest-need students | District-level, multi-state | Quote-based contracts | Deep clinical delivery; out-of-district-placement ROI | Student-focused; not organizational/educator |
| Cartwheel | Virtual student therapy, braids insurance + district funds | Growing (e.g., Bedford MA, $100K) | $15K–$240K by district size | Funding-braiding cuts district cost to ~1/3 | Student therapy, not educator/org health |
| Care Solace | Mental-health navigation/referral | National | $10K–$133K+/district | Light-touch, scalable, low unit cost | Navigation only; no consultation depth |
| RISE (Kaiser Thriving Schools + Alliance for a Healthier Generation) | Whole-school staff + student social-emotional health | National program | Largely free/subsidized | Direct educator-wellbeing competitor, free, credible backer | No clinical depth; not a tiered consultative engagement |
| CharacterStrong | Whole-staff PD + SEL curriculum | Hundreds of districts; 14,000+ educators rated PD | Quote-based | Whole-staff wellbeing PD at scale, strong satisfaction | A curriculum/PD product, not org-health consultation |
| Panorama Education | Climate/SEL measurement + surveys | Thousands of districts | Per-district SaaS | Owns the measurement layer WISE's Tier-1 needs | Data, not intervention or clinical |

Sources: [Civic IQ K-12 MH market, Jan 2026](https://blogs.civiciq.com/2026/01/13/k-12-mental-health-software-vendors-pricing-school-district-contracts-2026/); [Effective School Solutions](https://effectiveschoolsolutions.com/); [Cartwheel](https://www.cartwheel.org/for-schools); [Care Solace](https://www.caresolace.org/services/k12-education); [Kaiser Thriving Schools / RISE](https://thrivingschools.kaiserpermanente.org/priorities/teacher-and-staff-well-being/); [CharacterStrong](https://characterstrong.com/).

The pattern is clear: the *well-funded* players are all student-facing, and the *educator-facing* players are either free (RISE, Kaiser, Greater Good) or product-shaped (CharacterStrong) rather than consultative and clinical. No one occupies WISE's exact position.

### Adjacent players

| Org | Funding/reach | Pricing | Notes (incl. any strain) |
|-----|---------------|---------|--------------------------|
| Hazel Health | Well-funded telehealth, national | $15K–$240K/district | Student telehealth; could extend to staff |
| Consult4Ed / Wellness for Educators | Small, niche | Quote-based | Direct educator-wellness consulting — small but on-target |
| Headspace / Calm for Educators | Consumer apps, some district deals | Per-seat | Self-care app layer; commoditizes Tier-1 "lite" |
| Generic district EAPs | Universal | $12–$40/employee/yr | The true incumbent for "staff wellbeing" (see below) |

The broader district-services market has seen real strain as ESSER-funded contracts come up for renewal without the federal dollars behind them — a caution on unit economics, and a reminder that "we bought it during COVID" is not the same as "we will keep paying for it."

### Workarounds and "do nothing" — the true incumbent

For *educator* wellbeing specifically, the real incumbent is not another vendor. It is (a) the district's existing EAP — cheap at roughly $12–$40 per employee per year ([EASNA via Spill](https://www.spill.chat/mental-health-statistics/eap-statistics)), generic, and low-engagement — and (b) doing nothing, or running a one-off PD day or a volunteer wellness committee, supplemented by free content from CASEL, Greater Good in Education, and Kaiser's RISE. WISE's hardest competitor is "we already have an EAP and we ran a wellness day." The organizational-diagnosis, tiered-blueprint, clinical-rounding model is genuinely more than that — but the buyer does not yet know it, so the wedge has to beat *free plus inertia*, not just beat other paid vendors.

### Credentialing analogs

The "WISE-certified consultant" play has good analogs. Teach For All — the brief's explicit model — is a network of 60-plus locally led, locally funded, autonomous, demand-driven partner organizations coordinated by a central body ([Teach For All](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teach_For_All)); it is the right shape for *scaling reach without diluting the framework*. For the *clinical credential* itself, the IECMH Endorsement ecosystem (state Association for Infant Mental Health affiliates under the [Alliance for the Advancement of Infant Mental Health](https://www.allianceaimh.org/endorsement-requirements-guidelines)) is the closest template — and is exactly what sibling venture Attune/Attunify is building, which is both a validation and an overlap to manage (§6).

### Pricing benchmarks

- EAP: $12–$40 per employee per year (EASNA).
- District MH-services spend: $4K–$11.6M per year; most vendors land at $15K–$240K; navigation (Care Solace) $10K–$133K+ ([Civic IQ, Jan 2026](https://blogs.civiciq.com/2026/01/13/k-12-mental-health-software-vendors-pricing-school-district-contracts-2026/)).
- External K-12 PD: ~$4.1B market (2020); in-service PD ~3.5% of district budgets, ~$2K+ per teacher.
- Clinician certificates/credentials: e.g., $375/credit (UMN IECMH).
- Pricing in this category is overwhelmingly quote-based and unpublished — CharacterStrong, ESS, and Cartwheel all use "contact us." This opacity is itself a Stage-2 finding: getting three to five real quotes and comparables is high-value and cheap.

### White space and positioning

The defensible white space is specific: no one credibly owns *the organizational and relational health of the adults in the building, diagnosed and built as a tiered system, with clinical depth and research authority*. Student-MH vendors do not touch it; free educator-wellness content lacks the diagnostic, consultative, and clinical layers; EAPs are generic and low-engagement; measurement vendors like Panorama stop at the data. WISE can credibly stand as the clinical-research-grade organizational-health challenger. The catch is that it sits *between* well-funded student-MH incumbents and free educator-wellness content, so it must justify a premium against both at once. The 12–18-month threats to watch: a Panorama or CharacterStrong extending "up" into org-health consultation, and the free RISE/Kaiser offering setting the buyer's price anchor at zero.

## 5. User / Payor / Referrer Map

**User (day-to-day).** Two distinct users. First, school and district leaders — principals, superintendents, student-services directors — who consume the Tier-1 organizational protocol, leadership consultation, and PD. Second, school mental-health teams and clinicians who receive the Tier-2 rounding and coaching and, in the credentialing play, *become* the product as WISE-certified consultants.

**Payor (writes the check).** Rarely the user. In rough order of near-term realism: foundations and philanthropy (grant pass-through, e.g., the KCRF-to-EPH path in Philadelphia); districts (whole-child and student-services budgets, plus Title II-A PD funds, signed by a CFO or superintendent on a student-services director's recommendation); states (SEA line items and MTSS academies, e.g., MA DESE); federal grants (shrinking — a need-driver, not a reliable payor); and Medicaid school-based services for the Tier-2/Tier-3 clinical components.

**Referrer / gatekeeper.** The Georgetown/MedStar/Thrive research halo; conference relationships (the source of the brief's unsolicited inbound); state associations and MTSS networks; and delivery partners like EPH who write WISE into their own funded plans.

### Buyer paths and the first dollar

| Channel | Who writes the check | Proven funding mechanism | Named buyer in motion? | Read |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------------|------------------------|------|
| Foundation-backed district pilot | Foundation → district | Philanthropic grant (cost-neutral pass-through) | Yes — Philadelphia / EPH | Credible first dollar; fastest, lowest-friction, but grant-shaped |
| District direct | District CFO/superintendent | Title II-A (PD), whole-child/student-services budgets | Implied (100+ schools) | Durable if it renews; 6–12-month cycle; post-ESSER budgets tight |
| State contract | SEA / legislature | State line item, MTSS academy | Montana State (univ-routed); MA (unverified) | Most scalable and durable, but long (12–24 months) and political |
| University partnership | University → state | State appropriation via university | Montana State University | Useful credibility wedge into rural systems |
| Clinician credential | Clinician (often grant-subsidized) | Out-of-pocket / employer / grant | No | High-margin scale lever; slower to revenue |

The credible first dollar is the foundation-backed Philadelphia pilot; the scalable dollar is the state contract. Sell to the foundation-and-district pairing first to generate the case study, then convert that into a state-level motion. The clinician credential is the margin and scale engine but should be sequenced second, once the consultation model has a proven, productized core to certify against.

## 6. Mission & Studio Fit

**How it scales the mission.** Strongly on-mission. It serves the workforce that supports children's mental health — educators and school clinical teams — and routes impact to children through the adults, the most leverage-rich version of Thrive's thesis. It extends the mission from clinical delivery into systems and organizational change.

**Fit with the $3–15M sustainable, non-VC posture.** Good, if productized. The honest SOM (§3) sizes like a low-to-mid $3–15M ARR business and explicitly does not require venture-scale capital. The risk is staying a bespoke consulting practice that is labor-bound and hard to push above ~$3–5M without linear headcount. The AI-leveraged-lean-team and credentialing/royalty layers are what make it a sustainable product company. Tech-as-support — decision trees, partnership-mapping prompts, accountability scaffolding — is the right call and consistent with the studio's AI-leveraged-but-not-AI-replacing principle.

**Capital / outcome lean.** The brief leans spin-out (license the framework and IP, Thrive holds equity, no VC). That is plausible, but two facts pull toward seriously testing spin-in: the offering depends on ongoing Georgetown/MedStar clinical delivery and research, and the brand and likely the IP sit under MedStar WISE rather than the Thrive Center. This is the Thrive Academy pattern — something that looked spin-out-able but became a durable internal Center program. A "Wise spinout" also already appears on the studio's on-hold list, so the committee has touched this before. Treat spin-out as the hypothesis but make the spin-out-vs-spin-in call an explicit Stage-2 deliverable, not a default.

**Sibling-venture positioning — the most important internal item.** Attune/Attunify (IECMH consultation for ages 0–5; Neal Horen as clinical director; a consultation + credentialing + Georgetown-research model under a "Beside By Thrive" go-to-market) is a structural near-twin of this venture, as the brief itself recognizes. The overlap is sharpest on the credentialing/clinician-training and shared-services layers — research engine, credential infrastructure, brand architecture, and in some states the same buyer relationships. The clean split is by population and setting: Attune is 0–5 / early childhood / IECMH; WISE is K–12 / school staff / MTSS. But that split must be drawn *before* either goes customer-facing, or the two will collide in the same district and confuse the same buyers. There is also overlap to clarify with Virtual Consultation (Stage 2). This is the number-one question for the gate committee.

**What stays at the university vs. what spins out.** Research, the evidence base, clinical training, and the credibility anchor stay at Georgetown/Thrive (and MedStar); operations, go-to-market, and the consultation/credentialing delivery go to the entity; Thrive holds equity and is paid in resource cost-recovery. The unavoidable seam: the WISE brand and IP appear to be MedStar's, so the license has to reconcile a MedStar–Georgetown/Thrive–new-entity triangle, with OTC involvement. This is more complex than Attunify's Georgetown-only structure and should be scoped with OTC early.

**Verdict: fits with guardrails — needs internal resolution on two specific questions for the gate committee (Matt / Jason / JB).** First, where does WISE (K-12 MTSS) end and Attune/Attunify (0–5 IECMH) begin — neighbors, shared-services partners, or eventually one organization? Second, spin-out vs. spin-in, and who owns the brand and IP given the MedStar-WISE home?

## 7. Operator Priors

The brief names a clinical/academic lead (Megan) and a studio lead (Anna Jesseman), with the operator/CEO seat explicitly TBD and outreach planned to Nicola, Aubrey, and Sam. No public-record due diligence is possible without surnames; treat the named individuals as authoritative internal context.

The seats, separated in line with the studio's idea-bringer-is-not-the-operator model:

Comfort with the political and relational length of state sales cycles is essential.
- **Bridge (Anna).** A MedStar/Thrive boundary-spanner and the Academy lead — valuable for navigating the MedStar–Georgetown seam, but already stretched across Thrive Academy.

The one thing to test in operator conversations with Nicola, Aubrey, and Sam: can they point to a specific prior win selling a relationship-heavy, evidence-based service into *districts or states* — not just running a program — and are they energized by building a sustainable mid-size company rather than a venture-scale one? Posture-fit is the highest-risk filter here.

## 8. Answers to the Brief's Open Questions

**1. Willingness to pay — who writes the check?** A mix, but sequenced: foundations first (the EPH/Philadelphia grant pass-through is the credible first dollar), districts next (Title II-A plus whole-child budgets), and states as the durable, scalable layer (MTSS-academy contracts). Federal grants are a need-driver, not a reliable payor, post-2025. The honest caution is that every named buyer is currently *seeking funding*, not signing a recurring contract, so Stage 2 must convert at least one "inbound interest" into a quoted, funded commitment. Philadelphia is the right first case study; verify that EPH's economics support paying specifically for the educator-wellbeing layer.

**2. Pricing.** No public list pricing exists in this category — it is quote-based across the board, which is a finding rather than a dead end. Defensible Stage-2 anchors: $25K–$75K per school or $150K–$400K per district for tiered consultation plus PD plus Tier-2, and $1.5K–$5K per clinician for the credential. Benchmarks: EAP $12–$40 per employee/yr; district MH contracts $15K–$240K; external PD ~$2K per teacher. Recommend tiering by depth of engagement (Tier-1-only vs. full three-tier) and selling district- or state-level subscriptions rather than per-school one-offs, to build recurring revenue.

**3. Delivery model.** Hybrid, virtual-led. Virtual consultation is the scalable core and matches the studio's lean-team economics; a light in-person convening plus a licensed "WISE-certified consultant" credential is the durability and scale layer. Pure-virtual risks commoditization against free content; pure-in-person does not scale to $3–15M. The credential is the moat — but build it after the consultation core is productized.

**4. Clinician capacity-building vs. district consultation — the bigger play, and the sequence.** Lead with district/state consultation (the revenue wedge and proof engine); add clinician credentialing second (the margin and scale engine). The workforce shortage (1,071-to-1) makes demand for trained clinicians enormous, but you cannot credibly certify against a method until the consultation model is standardized and evidenced. Both are real; the sequence is the answer.

**5. Relationship to Attune.** Neighbors today, shared-services partners by design, possibly one organization eventually — but this must be decided internally, not left ambiguous. The clean split is Attune = 0–5 / IECMH and WISE = K-12 / MTSS-educators; the credentialing infrastructure, research engine, and brand architecture are the obvious shared services. The risk of *not* deciding is two Georgetown ventures selling overlapping "consultation + credential + research" stories into the same states. Escalate to the gate committee before Stage 3.

## 9. Recommendation (Go / No-Go)

**CONDITIONAL GO to Stage 2 (customer discovery + prototyping). Likely path: spin-out with guardrails; spin-in to be explicitly ruled out, not defaulted past.**

### Signals supporting GO

1. Excellent, leverage-rich mission fit — the workforce-that-supports-children thesis in its most resonant form.
2. A sharp, dated why-now — the ESSER cliff plus the ~$1B grant cancellation plus a 20-year workforce shortage create real demand for a build-local-capacity model.
3. Genuine inbound demand — multiple unsolicited district and state approaches, and a fundable first case study in Philadelphia/EPH.
4. Defensible white space — no one credibly owns clinical-research-grade *organizational* educator wellbeing as a tiered system.
5. Posture fit — sizes like a sustainable, non-VC $3–15M business, and a structurally proven studio twin (Attunify) de-risks the model.

### Yellow flags (ranked by how fast the venture dies if true)

1. Willingness-to-pay is grant-dependent and unproven as recurring revenue. If "inbound interest" does not convert to a funded, repeatable contract, there is no business. Fastest kill.
2. The headline evidence claim is not publicly verifiable. If the research-authority moat cannot be substantiated, the differentiation collapses to "another wellness vendor."
3. The model may be labor-bound. If consultation cannot be productized and a credential royalty layer stood up, it caps as a sub-$5M consulting shop.
4. The Attune overlap and the MedStar/Thrive brand-IP seam are unresolved. If not settled, two ventures collide and the spin-out structure stalls.
5. "No competitor" is false, and the buyer's true alternative is free plus inertia (the EAP and the wellness day). The wedge must beat zero, not just beat other vendors.

### Recommended next steps (cheapest, highest-leverage first)

1. Verify the evidence base internally (about a week, near-free): get Megan's published or in-progress studies on the educator → culture → student-wellbeing chain. This gates everything.
2. Settle the two internal questions at the gate committee — the Attune boundary, and spin-out-vs-spin-in plus WISE brand/IP ownership (MedStar–Georgetown–entity) — pulling in Neal and Shai (Attunify) and OTC.
3. Get three to five real price quotes and comparables, and pressure-test the Philadelphia economics: does EPH (or a district) actually pay for the educator-wellbeing layer, at what number, on what funding?
4. Run customer-discovery interviews with 8–10 of the "100+ inbound" schools and states to separate politeness from funded intent and to find the sharpest wedge against EAP/free-content inertia.
5. Prototype the productization and the credential — the decision-tree and partnership-mapping scaffolding, plus a draft "WISE-certified consultant" curriculum — to test the path off the labor ceiling.

### Open research questions for Stage 3 (customer discovery)

- Of the 100+ inbound schools, how many have *funded* intent vs. interest, and through which mechanism?
- What is the real per-school, per-district, and per-state price ceiling, by engagement depth?
- Does the district buyer value *educator* wellbeing enough to pay, or only *student* outcomes — i.e., must WISE sell the spillover, and can it prove it?
- Will clinicians pay for a WISE credential, and at what price, given the IECMH-endorsement comparables?
- Can a state MTSS-academy contract (MA-style) be won without displacing the state's own consortium?
